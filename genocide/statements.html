These resistance movements have a longer history of engagement with alien rule, and can be said to shape Kashmiri imaginations of azaadi or freedom from Indian colonial rule (Hussain 2018). This is why it is important to pay attention to the context of “Kashmir’s modern his- tory” which begins in 1846 through British acquisition of the regions occupied by the Sikh Empire (Zutshi 2018: 7). The details of this period help explain the context to Indian exercise of colonial sovereignty in relation to Indian-occupied Kashmir, which began in 1947 during the emergence of the newly formed states of India and Pakistan (an outcome of anti-colonial freedom movements against the British on the subcontinent). Some recounting of this history is necessary as it sheds light on the transfer of forms of colonial sovereignty where it concerned the region of Kashmir from one elite rule to the next. The 1846 Treaty of Amritsar sealed the British East India Company’s sale of an entire region to a Dogra feudatory of the Sikh empire, Gulab Singh, whose betrayal of the Sikhs helped the British defeat the Sikh empire (Singh 1971). This region included Gilgit Baltistan and Azad Kashmir (now in Pakistan-administered Kashmir), Aksai Chin in East Ladakh (a region occupied by China), the Kashmir Valley, Ladakh and Jammu (now occupied by India). Inshah Malik points to Kashmiri resistance under a Sikh empire governor against the Dogra army’s advance into Srinagar (2019: 24). As Malik states, “the Dogra Army had to seek British armed intervention to quash this rebellion and Kashmiris were in no position to resist the imperial army” (Malik 2019: 24).
